My current PhD work focuses on restoring lower urinary tract function. About 1 in 5 people will experience impaired bladder function, but despite this widespread impact it is often overlooked. Poor bladder function can come from many neurological conditions, including spinal cord injury, Parkinson's Disease, and more. Below, I highlight several specific projects I have worked on in this lab and previously. Selective recruitment of lower urinary tract nerves with epidural spinal cord stimulation

Although nerve stimulation has been explored as an intervention to improve bladder control, placing nerve cuffs for stimulation clinically would be a difficult procedure to distribute widely. Epidural spinal cord stimulation, however, is a common procedure. Here, we show that it is possible to access the pelvic and pudendal nerves that innervate the lower urinary tract directly with epidural spinal cord stimulation. Additionally, using a high-density electrode array we are typically able to find electrodes that will recruit these nerves selectively (to the exclusion of any other instrumented nerves).
